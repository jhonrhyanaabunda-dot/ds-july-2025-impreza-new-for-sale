01From the sales floor

Why the 2025 Impreza fits the way people actually drive here.

The 2025 Impreza is the entry point into the Subaru lineup, and it punches above its class on the things compact-car shoppers actually care about: standard all-wheel drive, hatchback practicality, and Subaru's reputation for going the distance.

Every 2025 Impreza comes with Subaru's Symmetrical All-Wheel Drive standard, not an upcharge, not a higher trim, which is rare in the compact hatchback segment and a real advantage for San Diego County drivers who want surer footing on a wet coastal morning or a weekend climb up the 8 toward the Lagunas. It's a five-door hatchback now, with no sedan body style offered for this generation. That gives you more usable cargo access than a traditional sedan while keeping a footprint that's easy to park around National City and Chula Vista.

A first car, a commuter, or a right-sized second vehicle

Pair standard AWD and hatch practicality with Subaru's reputation for holding up over the long haul, and the Impreza makes a strong case as a first new car, a reliable commuter, or a right-sized second vehicle for the household. When the first 2025 units arrived on our lot, our team walked each one off the truck and matched the window stickers against the build sheets before they went online, so the trim, the package, and the standard equipment you see at subarudalton.com are the car that's actually parked here, not a stock photo from a brochure.

The contrarian part nobody else will tell you

The Impreza is the right answer for most compact shoppers precisely because it nails the balance, AWD, efficiency, hatch practicality, and Subaru safety, in a footprint that parks easily. If you genuinely need more ground clearance, more seats, or outright speed, we'd rather sell you the right car the first time. That's the standard we hold this whole page to.

02The lineup

2025 Impreza trims, and what actually changes between them.

Subaru built the 2025 Impreza around three trims. Here's how we describe them on the lot when a shopper asks us to cut through the spec sheet, and who each one is actually for.

2.0LValue pick

Base

The straightforward commuter. Standard Symmetrical AWD, dual 7-inch infotainment screens — not just on "most" builds, but completely standard on 100% of Base trims — and the EyeSight driver-assist suite. The trim first-time buyers and budget-minded households gravitate toward, and it does not feel stripped.

2.0LMiddle ground

Sport

A little more presence. Adds the visual and trim upgrades, sportier seats, paddle shifters, body-color exterior accents, and the single, large 11.6-inch vertical Subaru Multimedia touchscreen. The pick for buyers who want more without jumping to the top.

2.5LPerformance

RS

The performance-leaning trim. Paired with the larger 2.5-liter engine instead of the base 2.0-liter. If you want the most pull merging onto the 805 or the 5, this is the one we steer enthusiasts toward.

The honest read

The gap between Base and Sport is mostly cosmetic and convenience. The gap between Sport and RS is mechanical, because of the engine. We tell shoppers to drive both engines back-to-back before deciding: most people who think they need the RS are happy with the 2.0-liter, and the few who aren't know within the first on-ramp.

Standard on every trim

No matter where you land in the lineup, every 2025 Impreza shares the hardware Subaru is known for: standard Symmetrical All-Wheel Drive, the EyeSight Driver Assist suite, the five-door hatchback body, and a continuously variable transmission. AWD is never an optional package on any Impreza trim, including the Base.

04Safety

Safety: what the ratings actually say.

Subaru's long-standing focus on safety is a core reason buyers choose the brand, and the 2025 Impreza ships with the EyeSight Driver Assist suite, which bundles adaptive cruise control, lane-keep assist, and pre-collision braking, standard across the lineup.

Don't take our word for it

Independent crash-test data is worth checking before any purchase. The Insurance Institute for Highway Safety (iihs.org) and the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (nhtsa.gov) both publish ratings and any open recall information by VIN. For first-time new-car buyers and families putting a new driver behind the wheel, that reputation carries real weight.

We walk you straight to the data

Because the EyeSight cameras sit at the top of the windshield — and the updated system adds a third, wide-angle mono camera positioned centrally between the traditional dual stereoscopic lenses — a replaced or misaligned windshield can throw the driver-assist off, so we confirm the calibration on delivery. When a customer is cross-shopping on safety, we walk them straight to nhtsa.gov and have them run the exact VIN they're considering, including used and CPO Imprezas on our lot, so they see the federal record for that specific vehicle, not a generic claim. It takes 30 seconds and it's the kind of due diligence we'd do ourselves.

Inside the cabin

The dual 7-inch infotainment screen setup is not just on most builds — it is completely standard on 100% of Base trims, while Sport and RS builds come standard with the single, large 11.6-inch high-resolution vertical Subaru Multimedia touchscreen, so your phone connects and the controls stay where you can reach them. 06The most common cross-shop

How the Impreza compares to the Crosstrek.

This is the single most common cross-shop we see on the lot, because the two cars share a platform. Here's how we frame it for shoppers standing between the two.

Same drivetrain philosophy

Both come with standard Symmetrical All-Wheel Drive and the EyeSight suite, so you're not trading away safety or traction either way. Ride height is the real difference: the Crosstrek sits higher with more ground clearance and rugged cladding; the Impreza sits lower and drives a touch tighter and more car-like.

Price and efficiency lean Impreza

The Impreza generally starts lower and rates slightly better on fuel, per the EPA figures at fueleconomy.gov. Our rule of thumb: if your driving is pavement, commuting, and the occasional weekend trip, the Impreza is the smarter spend. If you're heading off the pavement or you just want to sit higher, the Crosstrek earns its premium. We keep both on the lot, so drive them back-to-back; the difference becomes obvious in about a mile.

The catch: who should skip the Impreza

We'd rather sell you the right car once than the wrong car twice, so here's the honest counterpoint. There are buyers the Impreza is genuinely not the best fit for:

  • If you need ground clearance and a higher seating position, the Crosstrek is built on the same bones but rides higher, and it's the better pick for rough trailheads or anyone who wants to step up and out rather than down and in.
  • If you regularly haul five adults plus a week of cargo, a compact hatch will feel tight. The Outback or a Forester gives you the room. The Impreza is at its best with one to four people and reasonable gear.
  • If outright acceleration is your priority, even the RS is a sensible-quick car, not a hot hatch. Buyers chasing raw speed are usually happier elsewhere, and we'll say so.

If any of those describe you, tell us, and we'll walk you to the right Subaru instead of talking you into this one.

07Long-term ownership

Owning an Impreza in San Diego County: what the first few years look like.

A sales page that stops at the test drive is doing half the job. A new Impreza comes with Subaru's factory warranty coverage, and routine maintenance is straightforward, and we'd rather walk through it now than dodge it.

The boxer engine and CVT are designed for long service intervals, but the two things we tell every new owner to stay on top of are oil changes at the manufacturer-recommended interval and tire rotations, because all-wheel-drive cars are sensitive to mismatched tire wear. Let one tire wear unevenly and you can stress the driveline, so we rotate on schedule and replace tires in sets, not one at a time.

Service stays in one place

Our Service and Parts department is led by Rick Griffin, and the team handles Impreza maintenance from the first oil change through the life of the car. Keeping your service with a Subaru store also keeps your maintenance records clean in one place, which helps the car's resale value down the line.

What it's worth in a few years

For an independent sense of what your Impreza will be worth later, valuation tools like Edmunds (edmunds.com) and Kelley Blue Book (kbb.com) publish projected resale figures by trim and mileage. Subaru's strong resale reputation is one of the quieter reasons buyers stick with the brand.

Does the 2025 Impreza come with all-wheel drive?

What's the difference between the Impreza trims?

The Base and Sport share the 2.0-liter engine and differ mostly in styling and convenience features. The RS steps up to a larger 2.5-liter engine for more power.
